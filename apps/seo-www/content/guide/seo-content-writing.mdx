## What your content should cover

Before diving into writing, you need to know what to actually cover. The gist of it is as follows:

1. Check what's currently ranking for your target keywords by looking at the top 10 results and any AI overviews
2. Pull out the headings and questions the top pages cover
3. Find the gaps. Figure out what unique angle you can bring that they missed.

Your goal is to cover all the most important things that the top results and AI overviews cover, then add your own spin. That's the baseline assuming that the results are relevant.

Once you know *what* to say, the question is *how* to say it. Here's our playbook.

### Bottom line up front

This is something that many content writers already do today, but seems to be more important in 2026.

According to [Ahrefs' research on LLM search](https://ahrefs.com/blog/llm-search/), Chrome only considers the first 30 passages of a page for embeddings. That means your most important content needs to appear early. Don't bury your answer underneath three paragraphs of backstory.

When someone searches for "how to fix a leaky faucet" and clicks your article, they want to know how to fix it. Tell them. Right at the top.

Every section under an H2 or H3 should lead with the answer, not build towards it. AI engines extract these standalone blocks to construct AI overviews and chat responses.

A trick we like is to add a TL;DR at the very top of our article or tell you exactly what you're going to get up front. Give people the summary in a few bullet points. It instantly satisfies search intent and if the data from conversion folks like [Edward Sturm](https://edwardsturm.com/articles/pareto-seo/) is anything to go by, it can boost conversion rates significantly.

### How do I get my content cited by LLMs?

Back up your claims.

According to [Pew Research](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/07/22/google-users-are-less-likely-to-click-on-links-when-an-ai-summary-appears-in-the-results/), users who encounter an AI summary click on traditional search results only 8% of the time, compared to 15% without one. Your content is increasingly competing inside AI-generated answers, not just search result pages.

So what does this mean for writing? Two things:

1. **Use verifiable data.** Stats, specific numbers, named sources, and links to research. AI models assign higher confidence to text with verifiable facts. If you say "most businesses see better results with X," back it up. Say "businesses see up to 40% visibility improvement according to [Princeton's GEO research](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735)."
2. **Don't keyword stuff.** This should go without saying, but it's worth repeating. Writing naturally with real substance is far more effective than forcing your target keyword into every other sentence. Google's had decades to catch this, and AI models care less about these games and focus more on semantics.

### How do I optimize my content for SEO?

We like to keep "SEO optimizations" to a minimum. As you as you are placing your primary target keyword in the title, the meta description, and in the opening paragraph of your article, you're probably good to go. 

Also make sure to answer the original topic you set out to cover. Don't start covering unrelated territory, it disrupts the semantic signals that AI models use to understand and categorize your content. 

Keep your sections concise — ideally 200 to 400 words under each subheading. This helps both mobile readers and AI extraction algorithms isolate key information.

Where possible, we also like to phrase your H2s and H3s as the exact questions users ask. "How does internal link sculpting work?" is better than "Link Sculpting Overview." The paragraph right after should give a direct answer.

### How important is Schema markup?

The verdict is still out on this one. We currently just include schema it because doesn't seem to hurt, but don't waste too much time here.

The SEO community is genuinely split on this. Depending on where you look you can find studies backing up your claims.

On one hand, a [controlled experiment by Search Engine Land](https://searchengineland.com/schema-ai-overviews-structured-data-visibility-462353) found that the only page to appear in an AI overview was the one with well-implemented schema. The page with poor schema ranked for keywords but never surfaced in AI overviews, and the page with no schema didn't even get indexed.

On the other hand, [some SEOs argue](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/markseo_seo-share-7424067359088455680-vNFv/) that schema doesn't help and does nothing for your articles

Do give the links a read anc form your own conclusion. I personally just implement proper article and FAQPage schema where appropriate. It cost almost nothing to add, and there seems to be no downside based on what we've seen.

## How to do internal linking

Creating good content is half the equation. The other half is making sure search engines and AI models understand how that content fits into your site's overall structure.

This is where internal linking, specifically **link sculpting**, comes in. We agree with a lot of what [Press Whizz](https://presswhizz.com/blog/link-sculpting/) says on this topic. Here's the distilled version.

### Think of your site as a hierarchy

Every page on your site falls into one of three categories:

* **Money Pages (Kings/Queens)** — Your commercial landing pages. These are what you want ranking for high-intent keywords. Think "plumbers in [city]" or "best SEO automation tool."
* **Supporting Content (Court)** — Blog posts, guides, and informational articles that build topical relevance and capture long-tail traffic.
* **Pillars/Guides (Castles)** — Comprehensive authority hubs that attract external backlinks and sit at the center of a topic cluster.

The fundamental rule: authority should flow from your supporting content and pillar pages *into* your money pages. You're wasting link equity if your most authoritative guide is pointing heavily at your "About Us" page instead of your key commercial pages.

> Say you have a landing page for "plumbers in [city]", and a blog post about "how to fix a leaky faucet". You'd want to link from the blog post to the landing page.

### Match authority to competitiveness

Not all internal links are created equal. Press Whizz's core principle is straightforward: **your most competitive keywords should get links from your most authoritative pages.**

Here's how to execute this:

<div className="fd-steps">
  <div className="fd-step">
    ### Build your target list
    Make a list of keyword variants mapped to your money pages, sorted by competitiveness (keyword difficulty, search volume, ROI potential).
  </div>
  <div className="fd-step">
    ### Build your donor list
    Make a list of all your relevant supporting pages and guides, sorted by authority (URL Rating, Page Authority, or referring domains).
  </div>
  <div className="fd-step">
    ### Match them up
    The most competitive keywords get links from the most authoritative pages. If you're trying to rank for an extremely difficult commercial term, the internal link powering it should come from your strongest pillar guide.
  </div>
</div>

This turns internal links into equity multipliers. Instead of randomly linking wherever it feels natural, you're being deliberate about pushing the maximum signal to the pages that need it the most.

### Get the anchor text right

Anchor text is the visible, clickable text in a link. Getting this right matters more than most people think.

The ratio we follow (and what Press Whizz recommends) looks like this:

| Anchor Text Type | Ratio | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| Natural / Brand / URL | 60–70% | Keeps the profile clean and safe. "Read the full report," "Fluid Posts," etc. |
| Partial match / Semantic | 20–30% | The workhorse. Builds topical relevance without triggering spam filters. "Our strategies for content creation," etc. |
| Exact match | Less than 10% | The heavy hitters. Reserved *only* for links from your highest-authority pages. "SEO content creation tool," etc. |

The key insight: exact match anchors are powerful but dangerous if overused. Save them for your absolute strongest pages. For everything else, use natural or partial match anchors.

A bad anchor: "click here"

A better anchor: "our guide to building backlinks"

A sculpted anchor: "our complete backlink playbook for SEO/GEO"

The last one tells both humans and AI exactly what the destination page is about before they even click.

### Keep links contextual

Links placed in navigation menus, sidebars, footers, or generic "Related Posts" sections are heavily devalued by search engines. Your internal links need to be in the body of the text, surrounded by words that are semantically related to the destination page.

Search engines don't just look at the anchor text anymore. They also evaluate the words *around* the link to understand semantic relevance. A link to your SEO tool landing page should be embedded in a paragraph that's discussing SEO tools, not dropped into an unrelated section about email marketing.
